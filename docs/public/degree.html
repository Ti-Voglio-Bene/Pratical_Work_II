Course description

Studying Computer Science at Insubria means you will gain a sound basic and methodological knowledge of all the main fields of computer science and its most innovative aspects. Special attention will be attached to planning and managing distributed and multimedia systems, developing and managing web and mobile services, managing data and data protection, automation of services in public and private entities, implementation and management of advanced smart systems.

You can tailor your learning pathway by choosing amongst a number of elective modules: this will enable you to focus your training, to gain skills that you can immediately exploit in the labour market or facilitate your access to the disciplines taught in the Second Cycle Degrees in Computer Science. Workshops are available from the first year, to enable students to concretely apply what they have learned. Placements are available both in the private sector and within the Department.

Graduates will have a broad range of employment options in almost all productions segments and have excellent employability rates.

Lessons are held in Italian.

FIRST YEAR

Algebra and Geometry

The course aims to provide foundational knowledge of basic topics in discrete mathematics and linear algebra, focusing particularly on the following subjects:

  1. Understanding and comprehending the fundamentals of mathematical language: sets, functions, equivalence equations.
  2. Knowing the fundamental properties of natural and integer numbers.
  3. Understanding algebraic structures, substructures, and homomorphisms.
  4. Being able to use and perform operations on matrices and understanding the basic elements of analytic geometry for the Cartesian representation of geometric objects.
  5. Solving linear systems using different methods and understanding the characteristics of linear systems through the use of matrices.
  6. Understanding the fundamental definitions of vector spaces and linear mappings.
This knowledge, besides being an integral part of the cultural foundation of a student pursuing a scientific degree, aims to develop the ability to abstract problems and information through symbolic and mathematical representation. The course will complement the more theoretical and methodological aspects of mathematics with technical approaches that enable the resolution of exercises, making mathematics a tool for understanding and computation in various applied fields.

Computer Architecture

The course aims to enable students to understand the design and programming of the building blocks of computers. The topics covered are selected based on a well-established tradition in teaching the subject, which views the computer as a hierarchy of real or virtual machines. The course aims to provide all the necessary skills related to:

  1. the fundamentals of electronic circuits (viewed at the logical level) and basic design capabilities;
  2. the organization of computer hardware;
  3. the functions performed by the hardware and utilized by operating systems and application programs at the memory level;
  4. the design of a real processor (MIPS machine).
Students will acquire knowledge and understanding in relation to:
  • the construction principles and characteristics of electronic memory commonly used in computers;
  • the construction principles and functional characteristics of cache memory;
  • the construction principles of processing units (datapath) and control units.

Programming

This course provides an introduction to computer programming using the Java language. The primary goal is to equip students with the knowledge necessary to understand and use Java and to apply the language to problem-solving. The course combines theoretical instruction on Java and its structures with exercises and labs. Upon completion of this course, students will:

  1. Understand the main elements that characterize a programming language (syntax, semantics, implementation mechanisms);
  2. Be familiar with control structures, basic data structures, and the main abstractions provided by Java and object-oriented languages in general;
  3. Possess the knowledge necessary to independently study a programming language and understand the issues related to the development and application of programming languages;
  4. Be able to apply the programming language to solve simple problems by identifying the most suitable abstractions for formalizing the problem in an object-oriented language;
  5. Be capable of identifying the conceptual framework for solving a problem and using the programming language to organize its solution;
  6. Be able to communicate effectively and with correct terminology the characteristics of a programming language and the structure of the applications developed.

ALGORITHMS AND DATA STRUCTURES

The course aims to enable students to apply foundational knowledge related to the main data structures and associated algorithms. To this end, students will learn the basic techniques for algorithm design and analysis, along with the ability to solve classic problems related to data processing. At the end of the course, the student will be able to:

  1. Understand the relevant characteristics of an abstract computational model, as well as the importance of an algorithm's complexity for its practical use.
  2. Identify the most suitable basic algorithms and data structures for a given application context.
  3. Know and apply the main paradigms of algorithm design.
The student will also develop a conscious ability to make judgments regarding the typical challenges of designing efficient algorithms. Finally, the student will acquire a proficiency in formalizing problems in a manner suitable for computational treatment.

MATHEMATICAL ANALYSIS

The objective of this course is to provide students with theoretical and practical tools in basic mathematical analysis, with particular emphasis on the study of real functions of a real variable, sequences and series, and Riemann integration theory. At the end of the course, the student will be able to:

  1. Appropriately identify and define the theoretical concepts covered;
  2. Understand and present with proper formalism the results and techniques introduced;
  3. Reproduce short proofs and provide examples and counterexamples;
  4. Use the main calculation techniques acquired to solve exercises requiring the study of functions and the nature of series, as well as the calculation of limits, derivatives, and integrals.
Moreover, by the end of the course, the student will have acquired a mathematical formalism and a rigorous scientific reasoning method, which they will be able to apply to the study of other subjects in the degree program.

ENGLISH

The course aims to develop receptive and productive skills in English at an intermediate-advanced level (B2 – Upper-intermediate level in the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages – CEFR) for both writing and speaking/listening skills, in addition to introducing students to the use of specialized English for computer science. In particular, the student should demonstrate having acquired the following competencies and skills:

  1. Use the main morpho-syntactic structures of English at an intermediate-advanced level in different linguistic registers;
  2. Recognize and use basic specialized vocabulary for computer science (hardware, software, programming, web, etc.) to read and understand specialized texts;
  3. Engage in conversations on general everyday topics or specific topics related to computer science.

SECOND YEAR

DATABASES

The course aims to provide students with the tools, models, and languages necessary for using, designing, and developing relational databases and applications for such databases. More specifically, the educational objectives are as follows:

  1. Understand the characteristics and main services offered by a database management system.
  2. Know and understand the relational model for data management.
  3. Know and be able to use the SQL standard, both for creating a database schema and specifying integrity constraints, as well as for manipulating and querying its instances.
  4. Know the main solutions for developing applications that interface with a database; understand the concept of transactions and the services provided by a DBMS for their concurrent execution.
  5. Know and use JDBC to create object-oriented programs that interface with a database.
  6. Know the main methodologies for designing a relational database.
  7. Know and be able to use the Entity-Relationship model for the conceptual design of a database.
  8. Be able to address all phases of designing a relational database, with particular reference to conceptual and logical design.
Knowledge of data management models and their related languages provides the skills necessary for the individual deepening of one’s knowledge and the development of new competencies. For example, a student who has successfully completed the course should find it easy to independently learn the basic concepts of a new non-relational data model or develop applications that interface with a database using a language other than the Java language covered in class.

INTERDISCIPLINARY LABORATORY

The primary educational goal of the INTERDISCIPLINARY LABORATORY A and B is the development and practical application of skills and knowledge related to the creation of software applications, acquired during the first and second years of the course. Specifically, LABORATORY A will be delivered during the first year (second semester), while LABORATORY B will be delivered during the second year (second semester), in order to gradually acquire all the tools and methodologies necessary for developing a real software application.

LABORATORY A:
The main goal of the first part of this course (first year) is to develop skills related to the creation of software applications, resulting from the application of the theoretical knowledge acquired during the first year of the course in the courses "Programming," "Computer Architecture," and "Algorithms and Data Structures." At the end of the first part of the course, the student will have experimented, through the development of a real software application, with the design of algorithms aimed at the efficient resolution of complex problems, based on data structures suitable to support the computational load of the problem at hand.

LABORATORY B: The main goal of the second part of this course (second year) is the integration of the skills acquired in the first part of the course, with the development of capabilities regarding the application of the object-oriented development process to design and implement software applications that interface with a relational database, starting from the requirements of these applications. This goal requires the application of knowledge acquired by the student in the courses "Software Design," "Databases," and "Concurrent and Distributed Programming."

At the end of the second part of the course, the student will have expanded their knowledge and skills (by adding functionality to the software application developed in the first part of the course) with the design and software development of a distributed architecture, interfacing with a relational data management system. At the end of the course, the student will have experimented and will therefore be able to manage the entire object-oriented development process, and in particular:

  1. Have a general understanding of the activities and artifacts produced during the development process.
  2. Be able to elicit and analyze the functional and non-functional requirements of the system.
  3. Be able to design the application based on the analyzed requirements.
  4. Be able to design and implement the database, and the queries for selecting and manipulating the data.
  5. Be able to implement the designed application in Java, managing communication in a distributed environment, concurrent access to shared resources, and interfacing with the database.

LOGIC

  1. Knowledge and Understanding
    The course aims to provide knowledge of the basic mechanisms of logical inference through the study of the fundamental concepts of classical propositional logic and first-order logic. This knowledge is intended to develop and enhance the ability to abstract information through symbolic representation and, therefore, the capacity to understand an abstract and symbolic scientific language.
  2. Knowledge and Understanding Applied
    Some applications of more practical tools, such as SAT-solvers and non-classical logics (temporal and fuzzy), will be introduced for program verification.
  3. Judgment Autonomy and Communication Skills
    The expected learning outcomes include the ability to identify errors in a mathematical argument and to have the linguistic proficiency required to state a theorem and describe its proof.
  4. Learning Ability
    Knowledge of the logical mechanisms of mathematical reasoning provides the acquisition of appropriate skills for deepening one's knowledge and for the individual development of new competencies.

PROBABILITY AND STATISTICS FOR COMPUTER SCIENCE

The course enables students to acquire solid knowledge and skills in the main aspects of probability theory and mathematical statistics (descriptive and inferential). By the end of the course, students will be able to:

  • Understand and comprehend the language and fundamental concepts of probability theory and mathematical statistics.
  • Know and apply the basic principles of combinatorial calculus to solve simple problems.
  • State and prove some of the main theorems in probability and mathematical statistics.
  • Build models of random phenomena using well-known distributions.
  • Analyze and summarize data sets.
  • Perform parameter estimations in models describing random phenomena.
  • Use the knowledge acquired to solve problems in uncertain conditions.
  • Express themselves rigorously on issues of probability and statistics, formalizing and correctly arguing intuitions both orally and in writing.
The course also introduces basic elements that will be useful for further studies in computer science. Acquiring the basic language of probability calculus will enable students to engage in subsequent self-organized learning to meet work-related needs.

SOFTWARE DESIGN

The objective of the course is to provide students with the necessary knowledge for the development of software projects that are fully satisfactory both in terms of functionality and other important qualities for users and developers. To this end, students learn both conceptually and practically software design techniques, the de facto standard language Unified Modeling Language (UML), and design patterns. In addition, students learn the fundamental concepts related to software quality and basic techniques for software verification and validation. By the end of the course, the student will be able to:

  1. Apply the conceptual foundations of software design.
  2. Model and analyze complex systems and problems through abstraction and formalization mechanisms.
  3. Recognize and use different styles of software design.
  4. Know and apply the most important and widely used UML diagrams, such as Use Case Diagram, Sequence Diagram, Communication Diagram, Class Diagram, Package Diagram, Object Diagram, Statecharts, Activity Diagram, Timing Diagram, Composite Structure Interaction Diagram.
  5. Know the main design patterns and their families.
  6. Apply UML and design patterns for software system design.
  7. Know and apply basic techniques of software verification and validation.
The student will also develop informed judgment to independently and in groups analyze a complex software system project at various levels. The student will acquire mastery of the specific terminology (sometimes standard and also in English) used in the field of software design and will be able to present the functional and quality characteristics of a software system project using the correct terminology, in order to illustrate its strengths and receive proposals for modifications and improvements from other actors in the software production process.

CONCURRENT AND DISTRIBUTED PROGRAMMING

The course aims to enable students to program concurrent and distributed software systems. To this end, students will learn concurrent programming techniques, distributed programming, and their related challenges. By the end of the course, the student will be able to:

  1. Understand the characteristics and operating mechanisms of a concurrent program (comprising multiple threads of execution); recognize conditions where problematic situations such as Race Conditions, Deadlocks, and Starvation may occur.
  2. Know and apply primitives for creating and managing threads, with particular emphasis on synchronization and communication primitives between threads; know and apply techniques capable of preventing Race Conditions, Deadlocks, and Starvation.
  3. Know and apply the main paradigms of communication between threads.
  4. Understand the ISO/OSI standard and the basics of the main communication protocols.
  5. Understand the characteristics of distributed systems; know and use sockets for communication between distributed programs.
  6. Know and use RMI for the development of object-oriented distributed programs.
  7. Understand the basics of event-driven programming in concurrent and distributed contexts and write programs whose behavior is based on reacting to events.
The student is also expected to develop a conscious autonomy of judgment regarding the recognition of typical issues in concurrent and distributed programming and the selection of techniques to employ to achieve the desired system behavior.

OPERATING SYSTEMS

The course aims to provide students with knowledge of the role, objectives, and main functionalities and characteristics of operating systems, enabling them to understand and develop basic software functions that can be used within the operating system environment. By the end of the course, students will be able to:

  1. Understand the historical evolution of operating systems, with the ability to grasp the motivations and needs that have driven this evolution.
  2. Know the main functionalities of operating systems and recognize which implementation of a given functionality best suits a specific application context.
  3. Understand the principles of process-based environments and concurrency, with the ability to deeply and thoroughly comprehend their implementation.
  4. Recognize synchronization issues among processes and develop code to address these challenges. This skill will be honed during practical exercises.
  5. Understand the principles of thread-based program organization and develop software structured with concurrent threads. This skill will also be refined during practical exercises.
The student will also develop a conscious autonomy of judgment regarding the recognition of typical challenges in organizing code into processes or threads and the selection of techniques to achieve the correct system behavior. Finally, the student will gain familiarity with the specific (and often standard) terminology used in the field of operating systems.

PROCEDURAL AND OBJECT-ORIENTED PROGRAMMING

The course provides the fundamentals of procedural and object-oriented programming. These two paradigms are explored through the study of the C and C++ programming languages, using concrete examples. The primary objective is to understand the internal mechanisms related to the implementation of a programming language.
The student will learn to:

  1. Develop programs in C with a full understanding of its operational semantics.
  2. Apply the basic and advanced concepts of object-oriented programming to develop C++ programs, making effective use of the language’s main features.
Understanding the operational semantics of the C and C++ languages equips students to independently analyze the features of other programming languages (both procedural and object-oriented). This is further supported by the acquisition of transversal skills, such as autonomy of judgment and critical thinking in evaluating design choices during the creation of a language. Additionally, the student will gain complete mastery of the terminology used in the field of object-oriented programming.

Third year

DATABASES II

The course aims to provide students with knowledge of the internal architecture of relational database management systems (DBMS). The objective is to equip students with the tools to gain greater control over the execution and optimization of operations on data managed by relational DBMSs (e.g., queries and storage). To achieve this, the course will analyze the fundamental technologies underlying the architecture of relational DBMSs, focusing on physical structures, transaction management, and concurrency control.
At the end of the course, the student will be able to:

  1. Understand the main physical structures and related management (e.g., indexes, access methods) used in DBMSs.
  2. Optimize the design and execution of queries.
  3. Identify and handle transaction management issues (e.g., deadlocks).
Applied Knowledge and Skills
The course aims to analyze DBMS architectures, enabling students to make the most of these systems. The acquired skills will allow students to define optimized queries based on workload and data volume and to efficiently integrate application logic with DBMS functionalities.
Autonomy of Judgment and Communication Skills
The primary learning outcome is to enable students to assess database design, the associated physical structures, and the functionalities developed and managed by DBMSs.

AUTOMATA AND LANGUAGES

This theoretical course aims to provide students with foundational knowledge of automata theory and formal languages, including the main limitations in computability theory. These skills are intended to enhance the ability to abstract through symbolic representation and to comprehend an abstract and symbolic scientific language. Such abilities play a crucial role in various areas of computer science, such as compiler development, modeling complex systems, and software engineering.
At the end of the course, the student will be able to:

  • Describe the main computational models (finite state automata, pushdown automata, Turing machines), their characteristics, and their computational power in terms of recognized languages.
  • Formally define the concept of computation in the various models studied.
  • Identify the role of different languages (particularly regular and context-free languages) in the compilation process of programming languages and in other basic applications of languages.
  • Identify and use notations (generators) for describing regular and context-free languages.
  • Appropriately apply recognizers and generators to the study of simple languages.
  • Demonstrate a proper understanding of the limitations of various computational models.
  • Recognize and use the terminology specific to automata theory, formal languages, and computability theory.

    • FUNDAMENTALS OF SECURITY

    The course aims to provide students with the foundational knowledge and skills necessary for designing and verifying mechanisms to protect data in information systems and networks. This objective is achieved by first discussing the main notions of cryptography. This includes the analysis of basic transformations in symmetric cryptography and the study of major symmetric encryption algorithms (e.g., DES, AES). Additionally, the principles of asymmetric cryptography are analyzed, introducing the most widely used asymmetric algorithm, i.e., RSA.
    Data protection during transmission is presented by analyzing protocols at various network levels: IPSec for network-level data protection, SSL for transport-level data protection, and Kerberos as an example of an application-level protocol. This structure equips students to identify the vulnerabilities of individual protocols, which is essential for designing a data protection system. The course also aims to introduce the fundamental concepts of access control systems, achieved by analyzing access control mechanisms in DBMS supported by the SQL standard.
    At the end of the course, students will be able to:

    • Understand the principles of cryptography, the fundamental network security protocols, and access control mechanisms. These provide students with a solid foundation to independently learn new tools and applications for data protection not covered in the course.
    • Assess the level of data protection provided by an information system, enabling them to propose solutions, where necessary, aligned with current security standards.
    • Identify the main organizations and institutions that release standards for data protection in information systems and networks. Additionally, thanks to the insights and references to existing standards, students are expected to acquire the terminology specific to the field of data protection.
    Understanding the principles of cryptography, the fundamental network security protocols, and access control mechanisms provides students with a solid foundation to independently explore new tools and applications for data protection beyond the scope of the course. Furthermore, references to standards provided during the course will help students independently locate supplementary materials related to the analyzed and other problems. This skill is highly valuable, as it allows them to stay updated in a field like data protection, where new standards and critiques are frequently proposed.

    TELECOMMUNICATION NETWORKS

    The course aims to provide the fundamental knowledge and skills necessary for designing and verifying a telecommunication network. The topics covered are selected based on two criteria: topics that represent essential knowledge underlying any telecommunication network and/or topics characterized by numerous real-world and current applications. The objectives are to provide:

    1. n in-depth understanding of how telecommunication networks operate, fostering the ability to identify and solve problems with a professional approach.
    2. The acquisition of transversal skills such as effective communication, both written and verbal, and independent judgment. The course seeks to stimulate students' critical thinking in evaluating solutions, enabling them to assess the validity of obtained results and justify them based on acquired knowledge using a systematic and scientific approach.
    3. The ability to understand, develop, and identify network architectures and communication protocols that best meet specified requirements, based on knowledge gained through lectures.
    4. Familiarity with the international technical language specific to the field, enabling students to engage with open problems and discussions in the scientific community and understand potential future developments.

    MICROCONTROLLERS

    The course aims to introduce students to digital microcontrollers and their practical applications. It will begin with an introduction to the basics of analog and digital electronics, providing the foundation for interfacing microcontrollers with the real world. Special emphasis will be placed on operational amplifiers and analog-to-digital (AD) and digital-to-analog (DA) conversion. Subsequently, the most common communication protocols will be discussed, and the main types of sensors (temperature, conductivity, sound, pressure, chemical, etc.) will be presented.
    By the end of the course, students will:

    1. Understand the basic principles of microcontrollers, analog electronics, and analog-to-digital conversion.
    2. Become familiar with external hardware devices and the main sensors that can be interfaced with microcontrollers.
    3. Learn to evaluate and apply microcontroller-based boards (e.g., Arduino, Propeller as typical low-cost examples) in real-world scenarios.
    4. Be capable of designing and implementing simple practical applications combining hardware and software.
    5. Acquire interdisciplinary skills spanning computer science, electronics, and sensor technologies.
    These competencies will enable students to collaborate scientifically and technically with peers in complementary disciplines and work in interdisciplinary fields such as IoT and scientific instrumentation.

    BIG DATA

    The course aims to introduce students to the conceptual and practical aspects of big data, open data, and statistical and Artificial Intelligence methodologies for data analysis and knowledge generation from the infinite flow of data that surrounds us.
    The course seeks to provide fundamental competencies related to:

    1. Identifying relevant data sources to utilize.
    2. Manipulating data effectively.
    3. Generating knowledge from data processing.
    Students will acquire knowledge and understanding of:
    1. The characteristics of big data, both at the hardware infrastructure and software levels.
    2. Big data processing methodologies, including real-time information analysis models.
    3. Graphical and visual representation of data.
    By the end of the course, students will have developed the following skills and abilities:
    1. The ability to identify the most suitable data sources.
    2. The ability to design both infrastructure and software solutions for data processing.
    3. The ability to apply the most appropriate data analysis models.

    AUTOMATAS

    The course on Automata Theory and Formal Languages provides students with a foundational understanding of the basic principles of computation theory and automata design. It also delves into the theoretical aspects of programming language design. The study of Regular Languages, Context-Free Languages, and Turing Machines constitutes the central focus of the course.
    In the Automata course, the fundamentals of languages, grammars, and automata are introduced, laying the groundwork for the implementation of compilers and interpreters. Additionally, the course explores the principles of automata design as a technique for modeling and solving numerous problems that can be expressed through a grammar (a set of symbols, words, and combination rules).
    To understand the limitations of computer science and the basic principles of designing automata, grammars, and formal languages.
    The specific objectives of the course are:

    • To classify languages according to the Chomsky hierarchy.
    • To learn how to construct and use finite automata as recognizers of regular languages.
    • To construct and use context-free grammars (CFGs) as generators of context-free languages (CFLs).
    • To clean and normalize CFGs.
    • To construct Turing Machines (TMs) as language recognizers and as theoretical machines for solving computable problems.
    • To understand that there are non-computable problems.

    Specific goals:

    • Create the structure of a website in HTML5 based on current designs and adhering to minimum accessibility standards.
    • Use and apply CSS style sheets to create proper and visually appealing environments, allowing for adaptability across various devices.
    • Employ the Bootstrap framework for front-end web development.
    • Learn the JavaScript programming language to add interactivity to websites.
    • Understand and utilize the JQuery library to simplify JavaScript usage.
    • Identify the different criteria followed by search engines for website optimization and ranking.

    SEGURIDAD

    Information Systems are an essential part of the daily management of organizations. The continuity of operations largely depends on these systems, which handle the information and consolidate the knowledge and expertise of companies. For this reason, the security of Information Systems is a critical component of any design, development, or maintenance activities. This serves as a guiding principle for ensuring the security of infrastructures and services associated with information processing and communication technologies.
    OBJECTIVE
    To understand risk analysis and management methodologies as a fundamental part of the study approach to Information Systems Security. This involves analyzing the business processes of organizations, the information assets that support them, and the main threats and vulnerabilities that a company may face.
    SPECIFIC OBJECTIVES

    • Understand how the evolution of information technologies has led to software being designed for distributed platforms and web environments. Given this scenario, it is essential to consider software security from the outset of a software engineering project by conducting a security requirements analysis and implementing activities that ensure compliance with these requirements.
    • Address software security by emphasizing confidentiality, integrity, and availability. This involves not only addressing the risks of unintentional loss but also malicious and illegitimate actions, confidentiality aspects, and intellectual property rights.
    • Understand the technical, organizational, and legal implications of the attributes of confidentiality, integrity, and availability in Information Systems Security. This is achieved through the presentation of specific cases that integrate technical knowledge with a comprehensive view of the origins of security incidents and the solutions proposed for them.
    • Approach Information Systems Security as a process whose technical and professional resources must be managed throughout the planning, design, development, implementation, and maintenance phases.
